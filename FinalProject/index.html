NYC Heartbeat

45 million bike trips. One year. What does the pulse of a city actually look like, and what disrupts it?

DTU 02806 Social Data Analysis & Visualization  ·  Final Project

Every day, tens of thousands of New Yorkers unlock a Citi Bike and ride somewhere. Taken together, these trips form a kind of circulatory system for the city: predictable, rhythmic, and surprisingly legible. The morning rush pulls riders toward Midtown. The evening reverses the flow. Weekends slow everything down and redistribute it toward parks and waterfronts.

This story follows that rhythm through three lenses. First, how the city moves: a flow network that shows the busiest routes hour by hour. Second, how the city organizes itself: communities of stations that exchange trips so frequently they form functional neighborhoods, often crossing the official borough boundaries entirely. Third, when the city breaks its pattern: a calendar of every day in 2025, colored by how far it deviated from the expected baseline.

The data is the full Citi Bike trip record for 2025: 45 million rides, 2,344 stations, every trip logged to the minute. No sampling. The city, unfiltered.

Act I

The City Breathes

Each line below connects two stations. The thickness of the line reflects how many trips traveled that route during the selected hour. Use the slider to move through the day and watch where the city concentrates its energy.

Top 100 station-to-station flows by hour, weekdays only. Line thickness scales with trip volume. Station dots are sized by total departures in the selected hour. Data: Citi Bike NYC 2025.

Two hubs dominate the map at almost every hour: central Midtown, and the cluster around the Financial District at the southern tip of Manhattan. These are the gravitational centers of the network. Thick lines radiate in and out of both areas throughout the day, with the density peaking during the morning and evening commute windows.

The pattern is not a clean reversal: it is more like a pulse. The same corridors that fill with inbound trips at 8am stay busy at 6pm, just with more mixing. Riders who commuted in from one direction do not all go back the same way. The city breathes, but unevenly.

At 2am almost nothing moves. A handful of lines remain, and the bright station dots shrink to near-nothing. The city does not stop, but it gets very quiet.

Act II

The City Organizes Itself

If you look at which stations exchange the most trips with each other, clusters emerge. Each color below is a community of stations that ride together more than they ride anywhere else. They do not follow the borough map.

Station communities detected using the Louvain algorithm on the full 2025 trip graph. Edges were weighted by total trip volume; only station pairs with at least 50 shared trips were included. Top 8 communities shown. Hover over a station to see its name.

The official map of New York draws hard lines between Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx. The bike data does not respect those lines. Stations in DUMBO and around the Brooklyn Bridge belong to the same community as stations in the Financial District: their riders cross the East River every day. The river is not a barrier. It is a commute corridor.

The algorithm found 14 communities in total. The top 8 each have a clear geographic identity: Upper Manhattan, Midtown, Chelsea and the West Village, the Financial District, the Lower East Side, Long Island City in Queens, central Brooklyn, and a southern Brooklyn cluster. But the boundaries are drawn by trip volume, not by zip code. A station on the wrong side of a park or a bridge ends up in a different community than its geographical neighbors.

These functional neighborhoods show how New Yorkers actually experience the city, not how the city is officially divided. If you live in DUMBO and work in the Financial District, you are, in any meaningful sense, part of the same neighborhood.

Act III

When the City Breaks Its Pattern

Every square below is one day in 2025. Green means more trips than expected for that weekday. Red means fewer. The expected value is the rolling average of the same weekday over the previous four weeks. Hover over any square to see the date and deviation.

Daily trip counts normalized against a rolling 4-week same-weekday baseline. Color encodes deviation in standard deviations. Data: Citi Bike NYC 2025.

The red squares are the city's memory. The single worst day in 2025 was October 13, nearly three standard deviations below baseline. Late October has a cluster of red days that almost certainly corresponds to a storm system. November 28 is Thanksgiving. February has scattered red weeks where winter weather kept riders indoors. The data does not know why ridership dropped on any of these days. It just records the drop, and lets you fill in the reason.

The green squares tell the other half of the story. Summer months are consistently above baseline, with the warmest stretch of the year generating the densest cluster of green. May has a bright green run that suggests a spell of unusually good spring weather. The algorithm compares each day against the same weekday four weeks prior, so seasonal drift is removed: green means more trips than you would expect for that specific day of the week at that point in the year.

The flow network shows the city's normal rhythm. The calendar shows every time that rhythm was disrupted. The city has a heartbeat. This is what it looks like when it skips.

45 million trips is an enormous number, but what makes Citi Bike data valuable is not its size. It is its granularity: every trip has an origin, a destination, a timestamp, and a rider type. That is enough to reconstruct the city's movement almost completely, at least for the fraction of New Yorkers who ride.

What this analysis cannot show is why people ride. It cannot distinguish a commuter who has no other option from someone who chose the bike because the weather was good. It cannot see the trips that did not happen: the person who wanted to ride but found no bikes available, or gave up because the route felt unsafe.

The data remembers everything it was told. The rest of the city is somewhere in the gaps.
